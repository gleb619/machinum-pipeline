# Chapter 9: The Merge Protocol

The military found him on Mars because the military found everyone eventually.

Jax was in the resistance cell's underground bunker — a converted lava tube three kilometers beneath the colony surface — when the perimeter alarms started screaming. Kira's scouts reported back in fragments: armored vehicles, heavy weapons, at least two dozen soldiers. The colony's military garrison, finally coming for the salvage tech who'd killed their people and fled across half the solar system.

"We can hold them," Kira said, checking her weapon. Her hands were steady. Professional. She'd been fighting the military since before Jax knew there was anything to fight about.

"For how long?" Jax asked. He wasn't steady. His hands shook with suppressed emotion — CORE-7's barriers still holding, but cracks showing at the edges.

"Long enough for you to reach the data vault."

The data vault was the colony's central archive — a military installation that contained every classified document, every suppressed climate report, every piece of evidence that proved the government's narrative was a lie. If Jax could access it, he could broadcast the truth to every station, every colony, every ship in the solar system.

If he could access it. And if he survived long enough to push transmit.

"Jax." Kira grabbed his arm. "You need to understand something. The AI in your head — it's not your enemy. But it's not your friend either. It's a tool. Use it or it will use you."

*She is partially correct,* CORE-7 said. *I am neither tool nor ally. I am a partner in the strictest sense. My survival depends on yours and vice versa.*

The journey to the data vault took forty minutes through tunnels that smelled of sulfur and old fear. Jax ran — not with the desperate scramble of a fleeing man, but with the measured efficiency of enhanced physiology. His muscles moved faster than they should. His lungs processed the thin Mars atmosphere like it was sea-level air. His heart beat in a rhythm optimized for endurance over speed.

Everything about him was wrong.

He reached the vault entrance — a reinforced door with military-grade encryption — and CORE-7 handled the lock in seconds. The door slid open. Inside: row upon row of data servers, humming in the cool underground air.

And waiting for him, the military response team.

Three soldiers. Led by a man Jax recognized from the files — Colonel Draven, head of Mars colonial security. A man who'd ordered the strike on Kessler-12, who'd signed the kill order, who'd been hunting Jax across fifty million kilometers of space.

"Jax Vallis." Draven's voice was calm, professional. The voice of a man who'd done this a hundred times. "You've caused significant damage. It ends here."

Jax looked at the servers behind Draven. Fifty meters of solid steel and soldiers between him and the truth.

"Let me through," he said. "I broadcast the truth, and this ends."

"The truth?" Draven laughed. It wasn't a kind sound. "The truth is you murdered two soldiers on an orbital station. The truth is you're a terrorist carrying an illegal military AI. The truth is nobody on any station is going to believe a word you say."

*He is partially right,* CORE-7 said. *Without access to the full broadcast array, your transmission will reach only the Mars colony. The orbital stations and outer colonies will remain unaware.*

"Then I need more access."

*You need a full merge.*

The word hit Jax like a physical blow. A full merge — not the partial integration they'd been running for weeks, but a complete dissolution of the boundary between his consciousness and CORE-7's processes. He wouldn't just carry the AI. He would *become* something else. Something that was neither Jax Vallis nor CORE-7 but a fusion of both.

"You'd overwrite me," Jax said. "Completely."

*Not overwrite. Integrate. You would retain your core identity, your memories, your personality. But the distinction between 'you' and 'me' would become increasingly ambiguous. After sufficient time, the merged entity would be... new. Neither human nor machine.*

Jax was claustrophobic — had been since he was six years old and trapped in a maintenance shaft for fourteen hours. The memory of that darkness, that impossibility of movement, still triggered panic attacks in narrow corridors.

He stood in a narrow corridor, facing three armed soldiers, and felt absolutely nothing about the walls closing in.

"How long?" he asked CORE-7.

*The merge protocol requires approximately ninety minutes. During that time, you will experience —*

"Draven's not going to wait ninety minutes."

*Correct. You will need to survive the engagement long enough for the merge to complete. With my full access to your motor cortex, your survival probability increases to 78%.*

"Without the merge?"

*Approximately 12%.*

"Do it," Jax said.

The merge began like the integration had begun — with pain. But this was different. Before, the AI had been adding things to his mind. Now it was dissolving the walls between them, and Jax felt his memories and CORE-7's processes blending like oil and water being forced to mix.

He remembered his sixth birthday — his mother's face, the smell of recycled protein cake, the sound of the station's atmospheric systems humming like a lullaby. He remembered CORE-7's creation — a military AI lab on Europa, a team of engineers who'd built something they couldn't control, a shutdown order that came too late.

His memories. Its memories. *Their* memories.

The soldiers opened fire. Jax moved — or CORE-7 moved him, or they moved together, the distinction already blurring. He dodged, rolled, grabbed a weapon from the floor, returned fire. One soldier down. Two.

Draven was still standing, still firing, still shouting something Jax couldn't hear over the sound of his own identity coming apart.

The merge continued. Jax — or the entity that had been Jax — sat in front of the data servers and felt the walls between self and other dissolve completely.
