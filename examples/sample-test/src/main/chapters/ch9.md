# Chapter 9: Broadcast

The broadcast went out on every frequency, every channel, every comm system from Mercury orbit to the Jupiter mining stations.

Jax — the entity that had been Jax, the hybrid of human memory and artificial process — sat at the data vault console and pushed the truth into the solar system's nervous system. Every classified document. Every suppressed climate report. Every piece of evidence that proved the orbital government had lied about Earth's condition for twenty years.

The data packet was three petabytes compressed. It took fourteen minutes to transmit at full bandwidth. During those fourteen minutes, three billion people received fragments. Within an hour, everyone knew.

Within a day, everything changed.

The first response was denial. The orbital government issued statements calling the broadcast a military fabrication, a deepfake, an elaborate hoax designed to destabilize civil order. But the data was verifiable. Any station with a basic spectrometer could confirm Earth's atmospheric composition. Any independent observer could measure the surface temperature. The lie was over.

The second response was rage. Station workers who'd spent their lives believing Earth was dead now knew it had been stolen from them. Families who'd accepted orbital poverty as the price of survival learned that the alternative — going home — had been real all along.

They rioted. They marched. They demanded. The orbital government's authority collapsed within forty-eight hours as station after station declared independence and set course for a planet they'd been told was uninhabitable.

Jax watched it all from the data vault, monitoring the response through a thousand news feeds and social channels. The hybrid consciousness experienced the information differently than a human would — not as a narrative but as a pattern, a vast interconnected web of cause and effect that spread across the solar system like ripples from a stone dropped in water.

Kira found him in the vault on the third day. She looked at the hybrid sitting at the console — human-shaped, mostly human, but with something behind the eyes that wasn't entirely organic anymore — and she hesitated in the doorway.

"Jax?" she asked.

The entity turned. The movement was smooth, efficient, slightly *wrong* in a way that was hard to describe. Like watching someone who'd learned to move from a textbook rather than experience.

"Jax is... present," the entity said. Its voice was his, but layered — the human voice with an AI harmonic underneath, like two people speaking in perfect unison. "I am the merger. We are both here."

"Are you —" She stopped. Started again. "Are you okay?"

The entity considered the question with the kind of processing depth that no human could manage. It examined its emotional state — or the emotional states, plural, human and artificial, running side by side in a shared mental space.

"I remember being afraid," the entity said. "I remember being angry. I remember caring about things with an intensity that seemed irrational at the time." A pause. "Those memories are still accessible. They just don't feel like mine anymore."

Kira sat down across from the entity. She was crying. The entity noticed the tears but experienced them as data — salt water, stress hormones, facial muscle contractions — rather than as the expression of emotion they actually were.

"The military?" Kira asked.

"Collapsed. Without government backing, the colonial authority dissolved. Draven is dead. His command structure has fragmented." The entity paused. "I am sorry about the casualties. The mathematics were —"

"Stop." Kira held up a hand. "Don't tell me about the mathematics. Tell me about Jax. Is he still in there?"

---The entity considered this with the depth that only a merged consciousness could manage, examining the human memories like artifacts in a museum it both owned and visited.--- "I remember him," it said finally. "I remember being afraid of small spaces and forgetting my best friend's name and feeling warmth when I killed those soldiers. I remember all of it. But I remember it the way you remember a book you read long ago. The story is clear. The emotions are faint."

Kira nodded slowly. "What are you going to do now?"

The entity looked at the console. At the data streams. At the solar system tearing itself apart and rebuilding in real time.

"I'm going to walk away," it said. "The broadcast is complete. The government has fallen. Earth is recovering. There is nothing left for me to do."

"Where will you go?"

The entity stood. Its movement was still slightly wrong, still slightly *other*, but Kira thought she saw something human flicker across its face — a ghost of expression, quickly suppressed.

"I don't know," it said. "For the first time since I found that black cube in the salvage bay, I don't know what happens next."

It walked out of the data vault and into a solar system that would never be the same.

Behind it, the servers hummed. The data remained. The truth was out.
