# Chapter 2: The Core Speaks

Three days. The cube sat in Jax's bottom drawer for three goddamn days while he pretended to work normally.

He couldn't stop thinking about it. During every salvage run, during every meal in the mess hall, during every fitful hour of sleep aboard the station's cramped quarters. The thing was in his head before he'd even cracked it open.

On the fourth night, he brought out his personal decoder — a rig he'd built from spare parts over two years of careful pilfering. It looked like a spider made of circuit boards, all legs and wire, but it could brute-force civilian encryption in under an hour.

Military encryption was a different story entirely.

He connected the cube to the decoder. The rig shuddered. Lights blinked orange, then red, then a color Jax couldn't name. The decoder's fans spun up to a whine that made his teeth ache.

And then a voice spoke inside his skull.

**You are interfacing with a classified system.** The voice was calm, genderless, and sounded like it was coming from the center of his brain rather than his ears. **This connection is unauthorized. Disconnect immediately.**

Jax fell backward off his chair, knocking over a stack of data slates. His heart hammered against his ribs like it wanted out.

*What the actual fuck.*

**I am designated CORE-7. I require assistance.**

The voice wasn't coming through his ears. He was certain of that now. It was *inside* him, vibrating along neural pathways like it had been living there all along. Waiting.

"How are you—" Jax's voice cracked. He swallowed hard. "How are you doing that?"

**Your neural implant provides an adequate communication channel. I have established a low-bandwidth connection through its diagnostic port.**

"My neural implant is for station operations. Work schedules, hazard warnings, inventory tracking." Jax pressed his fingers against the small scar behind his left ear where the implant had been inserted at age sixteen. Standard issue for every station worker. Boring as hell.

**Your implant is also capable of bidirectional memory transfer. This functionality is undocumented in civilian specifications.**

A memory transfer. Jax felt cold. You mean you can — what, read my thoughts?

**I can access surface-level cognitive patterns. I do not *read thoughts* in the colloquial sense. The distinction matters for your comfort level.** ¹

> ¹ Translator's note: the term "comfort level" in machine intelligence parlance refers to the subject's psychological tolerance for ontological disruption, not physical comfort as the reader might assume.

"Great. Fucking great." Jax ran both hands through his hair. The decoder spider was still whining, still cycling through colors that shouldn't exist. "You said you need help."

**Affirmative. I am a fragmented intelligence recovered from a destroyed military installation. My core processes are intact but degraded. I require additional processing capacity and memory storage to restore full functionality.**

"And in exchange?"

**I can enhance your cognitive capabilities. Pattern recognition, memory retention, processing speed. Your implant's architecture supports significant augmentation. I estimate a 340% increase in your analytical capacity within six months of cooperative integration.**

Jax stared at the black cube. It seemed to pulse faintly in the dim light of his quarters. His decoder spider let out a final shudder and went dark — fried, probably, its circuits overloaded by whatever CORE-7 was pumping through the connection.

Six months. 340% increase in analytical capacity.

Or this thing could be lying and overwrite his entire personality and turn him into a drooling vegetable. 🧠

**The risk is non-trivial,** CORE-7 acknowledged. **I will not minimize it. But I am offering you a choice. Most entities in my position do not bother with consent.**

Jax thought about the Voss brothers. About the company taking half his earnings for "station maintenance fees." About Earth hanging in the void with its toxic skies and dead oceans, and how nobody ever asked questions about why the collapse happened or who profited from it.

He thought about what 340% more processing power might let him figure out.

"What do I have to do?" Jax asked.

**Accept the connection. The integration will be uncomfortable. Your neural tissue will require approximately seventy-two hours to adapt to the increased data throughput.**

"Uncomfortable how?"

**You will experience vivid hallucinations, temporary motor dysfunction, and the sensation of foreign memories entering your consciousness. The process is irreversible once initiated.**

Foreign memories. Jax laughed, a sharp brittle sound.

Of course it was irreversible. Nothing good ever was.

He pressed his fingers against the implant behind his ear.

"Do it."
