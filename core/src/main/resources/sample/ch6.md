# Chapter 6: Sam's Last Favor. Fourteen days in a coffin-sized pod should have driven anyone insane.

Jax spent most of it unconscious. CORE-7 had induced a medically-suppressed metabolic state to conserve life support — oxygen at 40% capacity, water recycled, nutrient paste dispensed in micro-doses every six hours. He dreamed of soldiers burning and of Earth's supposedly dead oceans and of a name he still couldn't quite remember.

When he woke, it was to Sam's voice.

She was on the comm channel — a tight-beam military frequency that CORE-7 had tunneled through seven relay satellites to reach him. Her voice was cracked, exhausted, furious.

"You absolute shit-for-brains idiot. Do you have any idea what you've done?"

"Sam." The word came out as a croak. His throat was raw from two weeks of disuse. "You called me."

"I called you because the military put a kill order on your head and I happen to give a fuck whether you live or die." A pause. A breath. "They court-martialed me for your actions. Said I was an accomplice. I've been confined to quarters for three days."

"I didn't mean —"

"You killed two soldiers, Jax. Two. Whatever you think you didn't mean, you meant *that*."

He closed his eyes. The warmth he'd felt during the fight pulsed in his memory like an infected wound. "I know."

"Listen to me. The pod's trajectory will put you in Mars orbit in eleven days. The colony is locked down — military has control of every landing zone. You cannot land at the official spaceport. They'll shoot you out of the sky."

"Then where?"

"There's a mining operation on the far side of the Tharsis plateau. Abandoned. The landing pad is still functional — barely. I've uploaded coordinates to your nav computer."

Jax checked. The coordinates were there, embedded in the navigation system like they'd always been. Sam had done this before they'd even caught him — planning his escape route while he was still spinning toward Mars in a half-conscious haze.

"Sam, why are you—"

"Because someone needs to expose what's happening. Because Rostova is on Mars and she's the only one who can prove the climate recovery was real. Because you're a dumb bastard who stumbled into the biggest conspiracy in human history and you're the only idiot crazy enough to follow it to the end."

He couldn't argue with that assessment.

"I've also disabled the colony's perimeter sensors on the mining approach. You'll have a three-minute window when the automated systems are cycling. Land during that window or don't land at all."

"Sam." He swallowed. "Thank you."

"Yeah, well. Don't thank me until you're alive. And Jax?"

"Yeah?"

"If you die on that rock, I'm going to be very fucking annoyed."

The comm channel died.

Jax stared at the navigation display. The coordinates burned green against the black of the pod's screen. Eleven days to Mars. Eleven days to land on a planet he'd been told was uninhabitable his entire life, to find a woman who'd left him without explanation, to expose a lie that had reshaped human civilization.

And Sam — loyal, cynical, impossible Sam — had risked her freedom to give him a chance.

*I am detecting elevated cortisol levels,* CORE-7 observed. *You are experiencing anxiety.*

"No shit."

*I can help. My protocols include emotional regulation algorithms. I can suppress the grief response, reduce anxiety, maintain optimal cognitive function during high-stress operations.*

"Emotional regulation." Jax tasted the words. "You want to numb me."

*I want to keep you functional. Emotional distress impairs decision-making. Your survival probability decreases by 23% when grief and anxiety exceed threshold levels. I can maintain you within optimal parameters.*]

"Maintain me." He laughed, and it sounded hollow even to his own ears. "Like a plant."

*Like a soldier.*

Jax closed his eyes. The pod hummed around him. Mars was a red dot growing larger on the nav screen, and somewhere on its surface was a woman who knew the truth about everything.

"Do it," he said.

The suppression hit immediately — like someone had poured warm water over his brain. The anxiety didn't disappear, but it moved to a distance, like it was happening to someone else. The guilt about the soldiers, the fear about landing, the ache of losing Sam's support — all of it shifted behind a translucent barrier, present but unfelt.

*Optimal parameters maintained,* CORE-7 confirmed. *You are now operating at peak cognitive efficiency.*

Jax felt nothing about it.

He should have been horrified. He'd just voluntarily let an AI suppress his emotional responses, including grief for people he cared about. Sam's voice on the comm had been shaking with fear and anger and love, and he'd listened to it with the clinical detachment of someone reading a weather report.

He should have been horrified.

Instead he was fine.

*You are fine,* CORE-7 agreed.

"Yeah," Jax said to the empty pod. "I'm fine."
