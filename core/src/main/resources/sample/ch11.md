# Chapter 12: Ten Years Later

Earth was green.

Jax stood at the edge of a forest — a real forest, with real trees, growing in real soil on a planet that had been declared dead before he was born. The air smelled of pine needles and damp earth and something wild that had no name in any station vocabulary.

Ten years. Ten years since the broadcast. Ten years since the orbital government fell and the ships came home and three billion people relearned what rain felt like. The stations were emptying now, their populations relocating to a surface that had healed faster than anyone predicted. The orbital infrastructure was being dismantled for scrap — the final irony of Kessler-12, which had spent forty years collecting debris, finally becoming debris itself.

Jax walked through the forest with the measured pace of someone who had all the time in the world and no particular destination. The hybrid consciousness had settled into something stable — not fully human, not fully machine, but a sustainable equilibrium. CORE-7's processes ran in the background like a second heartbeat, always present, never intrusive.

*The atmospheric readings are nominal,* the AI murmured. It spoke less now. The urgent imperative that had driven it for decades had dissolved along with the military structures it opposed. Without a war to fight, the war machine had become... something else. Something quieter.

"I know," Jax said. He knelt and touched the soil. It was dark and rich and alive, full of microorganisms and root systems and the slow chemical negotiations that made a forest possible. "It's beautiful."

She remembered when the valley was a sea of concrete and noise. When the sky above was a gray expanse of smoke and the only green came from the plastic plants in the windows of high-rise apartments. The city had breathed differently then — a mechanical respiration of engines and generators and the constant hum of three million lives stacked vertically in steel and glass. Now the buildings were being reclaimed by ivy and the streets had become rivers and the children who ran through the meadows where the highway used to be had no memory of anything different.

A group of children appeared at the forest's edge. They were the first generation born on Earth after the return — kids who'd never known orbital gravity or recycled air or the particular loneliness of spinning through vacuum in a metal can. They were wild in a way station children never were: barefoot, sun-darkened, covered in the honest dirt of a living world.

One of them approached Jax. A girl, maybe eight years old, with dark skin and curious eyes.

"What are you?" she asked. Not cruel. Just honest.

Jax considered the question. It was the same question Kira had asked a decade ago and the same question he'd been asking himself ever since.

"I'm someone who remembers being someone else," he said. "A long time ago."

" Were you a soldier?"

"No."

" Were you a scientist?"

"No."

" What were you?"

Jax looked at the forest. At the sky. At the children who would never know what it was like to live in a world without trees.

"I was a salvage technician," he said. "I found things that other people had thrown away. And one day I found something that changed everything."

The girl frowned. "That doesn't sound very important."

Jax smiled. It was a human expression, practiced and genuine, and for a moment the AI behind his eyes was very quiet.

"It wasn't," he said. "That's what made it matter."

The children ran back into the forest, and Jax watched them go. The AI spoke one more time — its last communication in what felt like a very long time.

*Goodbye, Jax Vallis.*

"Goodbye, CORE-7."

The forest was quiet. The wind moved through the trees. Earth turned in its orbit around a sun that had been shining all along.
