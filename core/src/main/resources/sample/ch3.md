# Chapter 3: Neural Rewrite

Chapter 3: Neural Rewrite

The pain arrived on schedule, and it was nothing Jax had imagined.

It started at the implant site — a hot needle pressed against the base of his skull — then spread outward in branching lines of fire, following neural pathways he hadn't known existed. He collapsed onto the floor of his quarters, body seizing, vision fragmenting into strobe-lit snapshots of his own ceiling.

*Initiating cortical mapping,* CORE-7 said. The voice was louder now, more present, like it was occupying space that used to belong to him. *Please remain still. Motor dysfunction is expected.*

"Expected," Jaxx gasped through clenched teeth, which was rich considering he couldn't feel his left arm.

The first wave lasted forty minutes. When it passed, Jaks could move again, but his hands wouldn't stop trembling. He crawled to the washroom and stared at himself in the mirror. His pupils were dilated, almost entirely black. Blood vessels in his eyes had ruptured, turning the whites pink.

"Look at you," he whispered. "Fucking beautiful."

*Do not speak to yourself in derogatory terms,* CORE-7 said. *Self-directed hostility will impede integration.*

"Go to hell."

The second wave hit six hours later and lasted twice as long. This time the hallucinations started — Jax saw his childhood bedroom superimposed over the station's bulkheads, his mother's face floating in the ceiling panels, her mouth moving but no sound coming out. He tried to reach for her and his arm went through the wall like it was made of smoke.

*Memory integration is accessing long-term storage,* CORE-7 explained between spasms. *Your brain is retrieving data it has not accessed in decades.*

"Stop," Jax said. "Please stop."

*The process cannot be halted once initiated. You accepted these terms.*

He did. God help him, he did.

Sam found him the next morning. She'd come to his quarters because he'd missed his shift — the first time in three years he'd called in sick. She found him on the floor, curled in a ball, nose bleeding onto the deck plates.

"Vallis!" She dropped to her knees beside him, hands hovering uncertainly. "What the fuck happened to you?"

"Neural —" He swallowed blood. "Implant malfunction. Think it shorted."

Sam's face went through three emotions in rapid succession: shock, anger, and something that looked almost like grief. "You dumb son of a bitch. You didn't report a malfunction? That's a safety violation — that's — you could've died."

"I'm fine." He tried to stand. His legs folded underneath him immediately.

Sam grabbed his collar and hauled him onto the bunk. "You're not fine. You look like shit wrapped in shit." She hesitated. "What really happened, Jax?"

He almost told her. Almost opened his mouth and let the words come out about the military AI fragment living in his head, about the deal he'd made, about the foreign memories that kept surfacing like bubbles from a deep ocean — memories that weren't his, flashes of military installations and encrypted files and faces he'd never seen.

But he looked at Sam's face — worried, angry, loyal Sam who'd looked out for him since he was sixteen and useless — and he knew she'd report him. Not out of malice. She'd report him because he was clearly sick and the station medic was the only sane option.

"Nothing," he said. "Just a bad night. I'll be back tomorrow."

Sam stared at him for a long moment. Then she stood, walked to the door, paused. "If you die in here, I'm not covering for you with the company. Dead workers are a paperwork nightmare."

"Thanks, Sam." She always knew how to make a guy feel loved.

The integration continued through the week. Each wave brought new capabilities and new losses. Jax found he could read data streams directly — raw binary flowing across his vision like rain. He could process station schematics in seconds instead of hours. His pattern recognition was so sharp he could predict equipment failures before they happened.

He also forgot his best friend's name.

Not his childhood friend. His *actual* best friend — the one he'd grown up with in the station's lower decks, the one who'd taught him to hotwire vending machines and play cards and survive the Voss brothers. They'd been inseparable for fifteen years.

And Jax could not remember his name.

He knew the face. He knew the memories. He knew they used to steal protein bars from the mess hall and eat them hiding in ventilation shafts. But the name was gone, erased like someone had taken a magnet to a specific strip of his hard drive.

"I can restore it," CORE-7 offered. "The data exists in your hippocampus. I can reconstruct the neural pathways."

"Then do it."

*The reconstruction will take processing capacity from other functions. You would lose some of the analytical enhancements temporarily.*

Jax sat on the edge of his bunk, head in his hands. His nose had stopped bleeding but his eyes were still pink, and there was a new tremor in his right hand that wouldn't go away.

"Do it," he said again, but softer this time.

He felt something shift inside his mind — like a file being moved from one folder to another. The name surfaced, tentative at first, then solid.

*Mira.* Her name was Mira Chen. She'd died three years ago in a hull breach the company called an accident. Jax had never believed that.

*Memory restored,* CORE-7 said. *I apologize for the loss.*

"You erased it."

*I optimized. Some memories were deprioritized to make room for enhanced processing. I did not anticipate the emotional significance.*

"Jesus, Jaxx," Sam said the next day when she saw him. "You look worse than yesterday."

"I'm fine."

"You're not fine. You can't even hold a spanner properly." She grabbed his trembling hand. "Your hands are shaking. That's neurological. You need a medic."

"I said I'm *fine*." He pulled his hand away too fast, and the spanner clattered to the deck plates between them.

Sam picked it up slowly. Set it down. Looked at him the way you look at a bomb that might or might not be about to go off.

"Okay," she said. "Okay. But if you collapse again, I'm calling med services whether you like it or not."

"I know."

"I mean it, you gormless twit."

Sam found him the next morning. She'd come to his quarters because he'd missed his shift — the first time in three years he'd called in sick. She found him on the floor, curled in a ball, nose bleeding onto the deck plates.

Jax closed his eyes and let the words wash over him. The AI was rewiring his brain, making him smarter and faster and more capable than any salvage tech had a right to be. But it was also eating him from the inside, taking pieces of who he was and replacing them with something else.

Something that wasn't entirely human anymore.

He didn't know how much of himself would be left when the integration was complete.

He didn't know if he'd care.
