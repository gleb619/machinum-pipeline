# Chapter 1: Salvage

Kessler-12 smelled like burnt wiring and desperation. The orbital station had been spinning in low Earth orbit for forty years now, its hull patched so many times it looked like a tin can someone's  grandfather had been throwing at a wall.

Jax Vallis floated through the salvage bay, mag-boots clicking against the deck plates with every step. The wrecked comm satellite was bigger than expected — a military-grade bird, judging by the shielding. Somebody's budget had gone up in flames. Or rather, had been torn apart by the debris field that made this whole sector such a profitable hunting ground.

"Hey, Vallis!" Sam's voice crackled through the comms. Sam was his boss, a woman built like a cargo container with a heart of gold buried under three feet of cynicism. "Don't strip the whole thing. Leave the transponder array for the company."

"Wouldn't dream of it," Jax muttered, though he absolutely would.

He cut through the outer hull with the plasma torch. The metal peeled back like old skin, revealing layers of insulation and something else — a dense core wrapped in electromagnetic shielding. Military. The kind of hardware that made you nervous just looking at it.

Jax pried the core free. It fit in the palm of his hand, a black cube with no visible seams. Encrypted. Valuable as hell on the right market.

He slipped it into his jacket before the internal cameras could catch the reflection.

Not the kind of thing you reported to the company.

The salvage bay doors groaned open and a rival crew floated in — the Voss brothers, both of them wearing matching smirks and enough sidearms to start a small war. They'd been competing with Jax for the same wrecks since he was a kid.

"Look what we got here," said Lars Voss, his grin sharp enough to cut through vacuum. "Little Vallis picking through the scraps. Find anything  interesting today?"

"Just another piece of junk," Jax said. 'You wouldn't know anything about that."

Lars's eyes flicked to the torch, the hull breach, the empty space where the core had been. Jax held his breath. But Lars just laughed and pushed off toward the other side of the satellite.

"Suit yourself, salvage boy."

Back in his quarters, Jax locked the door and pulled out the core. It was heavier than it looked, dense with something more than metal. He turned it over in his hands. The surface was perfectly smooth — no ports, no labels, no serial numbers.

Just a black box full of secrets.

He plugged it into his workstation anyway. The terminal blinked. Error codes scrolled past faster than he could read them. Military encryption, top-shelf stuff. The kind of thing that would take a civilian decoder a thousand years to crack.

Or you could sell it to the right buyer and never work salvage again.

Jax sat back in his chair and stared at the cube. Somewhere in his chest, a small voice told him to turn it in. The company would reward him with a pat on the back and a warning about protocol. The military would take it back and ask no questions.

But another voice — older, angrier — whispered something different.

Keep it. See what's inside.

He powered down the terminal, wrapped the core in a dampening cloth, and slid it into the bottom drawer of his desk. The kind of decision you couldn't take back.

Outside the viewport, Earth hung in the void like a blue marble wrapped in white clouds. They said the surface was uninhabitable now. Climate collapse. Rising seas. Toxic atmosphere. The reason why three billion people lived on stations like Kessler-12, scraping a living from the debris of their own ambition.

Jax wondered if any of it was true.

He went to bed thinking about the black cube. It hummed faintly, barely audible, like something breathing in the dark.
